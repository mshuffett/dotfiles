## Two Sentence Description

Your two sentence description is a concise explanation of your company for investors. A great two sentence description signals you're a clear thinker and succinct communicator. This impresses investors and other useful people like potential hires or press.

### Recommended Structure

**First sentence:** Plainly explains what the startup does. Anyone who hears it should have a clear sense for what it's used for, and ideally by whom. Nobody can engage with your startup if they don't understand what it is.

**Second sentence:** Plainly makes people understand why your startup is awesome and better than everything else. Ways to do this: specific user story that sounds way better, experience the founding team has that emphasizes they're the ones to fix it, or even super fast growth.

### Key Principles

**It's not meant to be all-encompassing.** Think of your two sentence description like the trailer to a movie. Its goal is to pluck out just the most interesting things about your company to convince investors they should spend more time with you. Don't cram everything in — investors are impressed by clear and concise communication. The two sentence pitch is the appetizer, not the main course.

**Use plain English.** Sometimes founders try to impress investors by stringing together popular buzzwords. It usually sounds something like this: "QuantumAI is an AI-powered platform revolutionizing the future of business by streamlining complex workflows to drive efficiency for our customers." Don't do this. Use informal, simple language your mother would understand.

### The Three Most Common Mistakes

1. Making it too long
2. Using too many buzzwords
3. Failing to include traction

### Two Sentence Description Examples

**Stripe (S09):** Stripe is the easiest way for developers to process payments online. In just 7 lines of code, you can start accepting payments from customers in 135+ currencies without dealing with banks, compliance, or security headaches that normally take months to figure out.

**PostHog (W20):** PostHog is open source product analytics. Unlike closed-source alternatives that charge $30K+/year and lock away your data, we give you complete control, unlimited events, and the ability to deploy on your own infrastructure in under 5 minutes.

**Airbnb (W09):** Airbnb is a marketplace where you can book a home or apartment when traveling. You get 3x more space for half the price of hotels, plus a kitchen, local neighborhood feel, and unique stays like treehouses and castles that make your trip unforgettable.

### Two Sentence Description Formats

**Format 1: What we do + example**

> Airbnb is a marketplace where you can book a home or apartment when traveling for work or vacation. When you visit New York City with your family, instead of staying in a cramped and expensive hotel room, you can stay in a comfortable 3 bedroom apartment at the same price.

**Format 2: Problem we solve + how we solve it**

> Stripe makes it easy for developers to accept payments on their websites or mobile apps. We do this by providing a simple and well documented payments API that any developer can instantly start using straight from our website.

Note: Many companies screw up format 2 by talking about the general problem in the industry rather than the specific problem they solve. A hint you're doing this: you aren't starting your 2 sentence description with the name of the company.

### Creating Pictures in the Listener's Mind

Good descriptions create pictures. In the Airbnb example you can imagine visiting New York City, a crappy hotel room, and a great apartment. In the Stripe example you can imagine going to a website, getting access to an API, and reading well prepared API docs.

Bad descriptions use jargon instead of pictures:

> "We are a peer to peer sharing economy marketplace for homes. We connect homeowners with people who need short term stays."

This is so vague it will leave little impression and be easily forgotten.

## Vertebrae

Vertebrae are the core of your pitch — the 3-5 points you most want investors to remember about your company. Great vertebrae tell a compelling and memorable story about what you do and why investors should be afraid not to fund you.

### What Vertebrae Answer

- What are you building and for whom?
- Why hasn't this been done before?
- Why is it hard to do what you are doing?
- Why is this an opportunity not to be missed?

### How You'll Use Vertebrae

- They form the outline of your demo day pitch
- Key talking points for freeform investor conversations
- Foundation for your "intro blurb" (a paragraph investors see when deciding whether to take a meeting)
- Core of follow-up emails to interested investors after Demo Day

### Vertebrae Examples

**PostHog (W20):**

1. PostHog is open source product analytics. We're like Mixpanel or Amplitude, but open source. We help people understand who is using their product, and how.

2. Our first idea turned out to be crappy, so we pivoted and threw an MVP onto Hacker News. The result? The all-time most upvoted B2B software launch on HN. After just 2 weeks we have 400 deployments, 1,500 GitHub stars, and an active community of contributors. We're growing by ~20 deployments a day.

3. We believe product analytics should be targeted at developers. Going after developers by being open source means we get in earlier at zero cost and with zero info-sec process. Being on premise means we can scale to data-sensitive enterprises in a way no one else can.

4. James was a VP Sales and has sold $5M ARR deals before, so we can do enterprise sales. Tim was the fastest developer out of 50 at the company we spent four years together at before.

5. We can displace the entire product analytics, event tracking and — longer term — data warehouse markets. In the long run, there are 8-10 times as many developers as product people in the world. We can bring data driven decision making to every company in the world.

**Airbnb (W09):**

1. Airbnb is a marketplace where you can book a home or apartment when traveling for work or vacation. When you visit New York City with your family, instead of staying in a cramped and expensive hotel room, you can stay in a comfortable 3 bedroom apartment at the same price.

2. Brian and Joe met at RISD where they graduated with degrees in fine arts. Nate, who was Joe's roommate after college, made over $1m after starting a software consulting company in high school, and then graduated from Harvard with a degree in Computer Science.

3. When using an early version of our product to attend SXSW we realized the most painful part of staying in an Airbnb is paying the host. By accepting payment in our site, and holding in escrow until after the trip is completed, the number of nights booked increased significantly.

4. We launched 3 months ago, we already have $15,000 of monthly revenue, and we're growing 50% every month.

5. In the United States business and vacation travelers spend over 1 billion nights in a hotel every year. On average we make $20 per night booked on our Airbnb, which represents a $20B market opportunity in the US alone.

## 60 Second Pitch Guide

**Note for Open Batch:** The standard YC Demo Day format is 60 seconds. Open Batch 001 gives founders 2 minutes. The same principles apply — keep it tight, don't fill extra time with filler — but you have more room to breathe.

### Goals of Your Pitch

1. Get investors to actually understand what you do
2. Get investors interested in learning more

### Getting Started

Start from scratch. Don't reuse old deck materials. Reconsider any language you've used in the past to describe your company — especially if it sounds snappy, catchy, or like a TV commercial.

### Pitch Structure

#### 1. What do you do? (Two-sentence description)

Start with your two-sentence description. You've already practiced this.

#### 2. How much progress have you made?

The easiest way to make an investor care is to present a surprising amount of progress. If you've accomplished an impressively large amount in an impressively small time — investors take notice.

**The time context matters as much as the number.** Compare:

> "We have over $15,000 of monthly revenue."

vs.

> "We launched 3 months ago, we already have $15,000 of monthly revenue, and we're growing 50% every month."

The second sentence creates more excitement because it includes when you started and your growth rate.

**A note on traction — be precise:**

- **Revenue:** Real contracted money coming into your bank (ideally some already received)
- **Bookings:** Signed contract but no money yet
- **LOI:** Letter of intent, usually non-binding
- **Recurring vs. transactional:** Recurring = regular billing (monthly). Transactional = one-time.
- **Monthly vs. yearly:** If you have $8K MRR, you can say "$100K annualized revenue" but NOT "$100K ARR" (because it's subject to monthly cancellation)

**If you haven't launched:** Leave traction out and expand the closing section. For bio/hard tech, mention significant milestones — especially if faster or cheaper than competition, or never done before.

#### 3. How do you make money and how big is your market?

Be clear and jargon-free about your business model:

> "When you book on Airbnb, we charge a 20% fee on every transaction."

Explain your market **bottom-up** (not top-down). Show the math:

> "In the United States business and vacation travelers spend over 1 billion nights in a hotel every year. On average we make $20 per night booked on our Airbnb, which represents a $20B market opportunity in the US alone."

Investors want to understand your math: number of potential customers × what you charge × your margins.

#### 4. Closing — the most compelling thing

Your last shot to share the most interesting/compelling/memorable fact. Pick ONE:

- Something you understand about the customer/market that most others don't
- Your personal experience with the problem
- A significant and relevant previous accomplishment
- Something that changed recently (within 5 years) making your business possible
- Your personal expertise in this industry
- A compelling vision for the future

**Personal credentials matter** if relevant: world expert, relevant PhD, important role at a previous company where you observed the problem. Include it.

Don't list multiple things — it dilutes impact. One strong closer is better than three mediocre ones.

#### 5. Thank you

Say "thank you" when your presentation is done. Always.

### Pitch Style Guidelines

- Use short, simple sentences and simple language
- Split long comma-heavy sentences into short ones
- Remove every unnecessary word
- Speak slowly and clearly
- Don't try to speak faster to cram in more words — it doesn't work

### Complete Script Example — Airbnb

> Hello, my name is Brian and these are my co-founders Joe and Nate.
>
> Airbnb is a marketplace where you can book a home or apartment when traveling for work or vacation. When you visit New York City with your family, instead of staying in a cramped and expensive hotel room, you can stay in a comfortable 3 bedroom apartment at the same price.
>
> We launched 3 months ago, we already have $15,000 of monthly revenue, and we're growing 50% every month.
>
> Business and vacation travelers in the US book over 1 billion hotel nights every year. On average, we make $20 per night booked. This is a $20B market opportunity.
>
> We first realized our product could work during a design conference in San Francisco. All the hotel rooms in the city were sold out and we rented out 3 airbeds in our apartment for over $100 a night each. Our guests loved the experience and we made enough money to cover our rent.
>
> We are Airbnb, thank you.

### Complete Script Example — Friday

> Hello, my name is Allen, and I'm the CEO of Friday!
>
> Friday is like having a personal assistant living in your email inbox. Like a human, Friday learns from the emails you deal with, and handles them for you going forward.
>
> We launched just over 3 weeks ago, and we have [Demo Day MRR] in monthly revenue, and we're growing [Demo Day growth] weekly.
>
> Email is a huge problem, with over 1.6 billion people spending at least an hour a day dealing with their emails.
>
> Founders, investors, marketers, and those who generally spend a lot of time in their inbox love using Friday. The CEO of Product Hunt and the Founder of Eventbrite & A* Capital offload their emails to Friday and supplement the work their assistant does.
>
> You investors can fully automate reaching out to founders, booking meetings, and following up with them. If you emailed me asking to meet, I'm sorry, but I didn't personally write that response. Don't believe me?
>

## Single Slide Presentation Guide

Once you have the description written, creating bullet points for your slide is easier. Less is more — if your slide has too much text or anything hard to understand, investors won't spend extra time figuring it out.

### Required Elements

- Logo (top left)
- What do you do?
- How much progress have you made?
- How do you make money and how big is your market?
- Significant experience / expertise (if you have it)

### Format

- Aspect ratio: **16:9**
- 1-2 slides allowed
- Second slide can be a demo video (uploaded to YouTube)
- Total presentation including video must be 2 minutes max

### Key Rule

Bullet points on your slide should match your script in **content and order**. Don't have bullet points that don't appear in your script, and vice versa.

## Founder Bio Guide

Write a 3-4 sentence bio covering:

- Education and work experience of founders
- How the founders met
- Why you are passionate about working on your startup
- Roles of each co-founder (especially who does software/hardware development)
- Specific personal or work experience that led to this startup

### Example — Airbnb

> Brian (CEO) and Joe (CPO) met at RISD where they graduated with degrees in fine arts. Nate (CTO), who was Joe's roommate after college, learned to code during middle school and graduated from Harvard with a degree in Computer Science.

## Top Mistakes

1. Write a customer pitch instead of an investor pitch
2. Spend too much time prepping/designing your slide
3. Use too many buzzwords (platform, AI, ML)
4. Are too vague and don't provide examples
5. Not obvious who the customer is
6. Not obvious which geographical market you are serving
7. Using a graph that isn't pointing up and to the right
8. Using a graph that isn't labeled / titled clearly
9. Have bullet points on your slide that don't appear in your script or vice versa
10. Have bullet points in a different order than your script
11. Having a distracting background or image
12. Inconsistent spacing between bullet points
13. Not saying thank you when your presentation is done

## FAQ

### How do you talk about progress if you haven't launched yet?

It depends. If you are very early you can leave it out and expand the closing section. If you are a bio/hard tech company and you've accomplished a significant milestone, mention it — especially if you put it in context. Did you accomplish a milestone faster or cheaper than the competition? Have you done something never done before? Your statement should make the listener impressed.

### What if your product is too complicated to describe in 2 sentences?

First, the vast majority of products aren't too complicated to explain in 2 sentences. Second, one trick: instead of explaining how a product works, explain what it does or what problem it solves.

### What if I want to show a picture of my product?

If you are showing off an app or website — you don't need to. At a glance most apps and websites look the same and showing off a nice UI really doesn't help much. If you are showing off a physical product then consider adding a photo to your slide.

### What if I have no idea what my market size is?

It's worth some research — sometimes 30-60 minutes of Google searching provides a lot of valuable information. If it's still hard to figure out you can leave this out.

### What if my market is too small (under $1B)?

Consider expanding your scope. Can your product be used in more countries? Are there more potential customers you are not targeting today?
