Original

I've spent my career integrating systems that have naturally stayed siloed — government, nonprofits, corporate partners, communities. This inaugural role is that same work at city scale. The opportunity to unify three agencies under a single strategy and connect the arts to San Francisco's broader priorities — housing, economic development, neighborhood revitalization — is a genuine convergence of everything I've built, and I'm deeply motivated by it.

San Francisco is home to me. I run DrawBridge Arts — a nearly 40-year-old Bay Area nonprofit that has provided trauma-informed arts programming to 40,000+ children experiencing housing instability across 14 sites. I also curated and launched The Art of San Francisco: a 7,000 square foot gallery and civic gathering space at 2 Embarcadero Center. Through this work I've built partnerships across the city's cultural, corporate, and diplomatic landscape — Pixar, Stripe, BXP, the Canadian Consulate, Marriott — connecting the Bay Area's arts community to national and international networks. I work in this ecosystem every day, from shelters in the neighborhoods to the Embarcadero - and I see this work as a deeply integrated system, rather than distinctive.

My background spans the specific areas this role requires. In my first year at DrawBridge, I secured a partnership that more than doubled the organization's resource base and established research partnerships with UC Berkeley and Dominican University to build evidence-based, measurable arts programming. At Scouts Canada, I led the first organization-wide culture strategy across 50,000 members, raised millions in grants and partnerships, and increased engagement 33% year-over-year — with four Commonwealth nations looking to our model. In government — for elected American Indian officials, the Government of Ontario, and Chiefs of Ontario — I supervised 150+ staff over five years, presented fiscal analysis for $500M in budget decisions, and co-led policy reform impacting 60,000 public servants. My long-standing work with tribal nations required a discipline in listening-first, ground-up policy design that I carry into everything I do.

The arts sector has spent decades making its case to people who already believe. I believe the real opportunity in this role is making every other sector in San Francisco — tech, healthcare, housing, education — see that the arts solve problems they can't solve alone. Rather than a department that advocates for the arts, this is an opportunity to build the arts as essential to everything else this city is trying to do. That requires someone without allegiances to any one agency, with genuine curiosity about all three, and with the cross-sector fluency to build those bridges. That's the career I've built, and I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how it serves the vision the Mayor's office is advancing.

Final

Integrating systems that stay siloed — government, nonprofits, corporate partners, communities — has been the through-line of my career. This inaugural role is that work at city scale. Unifying the Arts Commission, Grants for the Arts, and the Film Commission under a single strategy — while connecting that new organization to San Francisco's broader priorities in housing, economic development, and neighborhood revitalization — is where everything I've built converges.

San Francisco is home to me. I run DrawBridge Arts — a nearly 40-year-old Bay Area nonprofit reaching 40,000+ children experiencing housing instability across 14 sites. Our work integrates trauma-informed arts programming rooted in cultural equity into the housing system. I curated and launched The Art of San Francisco: a 7,000 square foot gallery and civic gathering space at 2 Embarcadero Center. Through both, I've built partnerships across the city's cultural, corporate, and diplomatic landscape — Pixar, Stripe, BXP, the Canadian Consulate, Marriott.

Every stage of my career demanded the same discipline: building coalitions across institutional lines, stewarding budgets, and earning community trust before redesigning systems. In my first year at DrawBridge, I secured a partnership that more than doubled the organization's resource base and launched research collaborations with UC Berkeley and Dominican University for evidence-based programming. At Scouts Canada, I led the first change strategy across 50,000 members, raised millions, and grew engagement 33% year-over-year — four Commonwealth nations adopted our model. Five years in government — for elected American Indian officials, the Government of Ontario, and Chiefs of Ontario — meant supervising 150+ staff, presenting fiscal analysis for $500M in budget decisions, and co-leading policy reform impacting 60,000 public servants. My work with tribal nations — grounded in their self-determination — instilled a discipline I carry everywhere: listen first, design with the community.

San Francisco has lost more than 30% of its arts organizations since 2019 — cultural displacement on a scale that threatens the city's identity. The opportunity is not more advocacy to the already convinced, but persuasion: making every other sector — tech, healthcare, housing, education — see that the arts, generating $1.8 billion in economic activity and a 9:1 return on public investment, are essential infrastructure. That case requires someone with allegiance to no single agency and the cross-sector fluency to make all three work as one. That is the career I've built, and I welcome the chance to discuss how it serves what Mayor Lurie is building.
