Good to have you back, Tanya. You've got your logistics squared away — RSVP confirmed, practice session on March 6, presenting in person. Now it's time to nail the two-sentence pitch.

Here's what you shared:

> "Roomie helps college students find compatible roommates. We match students based on sleep schedules, cleanliness habits, and social preferences — and 89% of our matches report being happy with their roommate after a full semester."

This is a solid starting point. Let me break down what's working and what needs tightening.

**What's working:**

- **First sentence is clear.** "Helps college students find compatible roommates" — anyone can understand that immediately. No jargon, no buzzwords. Good.
- **You included traction.** The 89% satisfaction stat is concrete and memorable. The YC guide specifically calls out "failing to include traction" as one of the three most common mistakes, so you're already ahead there.
- **It's actually two sentences.** You'd be surprised how many founders turn this into five.

**What needs work:**

1. **The second sentence describes *how it works* instead of *why it's awesome*.** Listing "sleep schedules, cleanliness habits, and social preferences" is the mechanism — that's a feature list. The guide recommends the second sentence should make people understand why your startup is awesome and better than everything else. The 89% stat does that, but it's buried after the how-it-works part.

2. **It doesn't create a strong picture.** The YC guide emphasizes creating pictures in the listener's mind — think about the Airbnb example where you can imagine a cramped hotel room vs. a comfortable 3-bedroom apartment. Right now your pitch is abstract. What does a bad roommate situation actually look like? What does a good match feel like? A specific example would land harder than a percentage.

3. **The second sentence is doing too much.** It's trying to explain the matching criteria AND deliver the traction stat in one long dash-connected thought. Split the jobs: first sentence = what you do, second sentence = why it's impressive.

**Here's a direction to try:**

Keep the first sentence almost as-is — it's working. For the second sentence, lead with the outcome that makes investors pay attention. Something like:

> "Roomie helps college students find compatible roommates. 89% of our matches report being happy with their roommate after a full semester — in a market where nearly half of college roommate pairings end in conflict or room-change requests."

This version puts the impressive stat up front and then contrasts it against the status quo, which makes the number land harder. The contrast creates a picture: "half of pairings end in conflict" — investors can immediately feel how broken the current situation is.

That said, I'm working from the guide here, not from deep knowledge of your market. A few questions that would help me give sharper feedback:

- **Is the "nearly half end in conflict" framing accurate for your market?** What does the bad-case scenario actually look like for students stuck with incompatible roommates? The more specific and real that contrast is, the better.
- **Do you have growth or scale numbers beyond the satisfaction stat?** Something like "X students matched in Y months" or a growth rate would strengthen this further. The YC guide says time context matters as much as the number — "launched 3 months ago with 2,000 matches" hits differently than just the satisfaction percentage.
- **Who is paying — the students or the universities?** This doesn't need to be in the two-sentence pitch, but knowing the business model helps me understand which angle will resonate most with investors.
