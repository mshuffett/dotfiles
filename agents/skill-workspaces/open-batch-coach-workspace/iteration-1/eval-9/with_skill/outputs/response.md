Good news: you don't need to fake traction. The pitch guide is explicit about this — if you haven't launched, leave the traction section out and expand your closing instead.

Here's what that means concretely for your pitch structure:

**What to skip:** Don't manufacture metrics. No "we've talked to 50 potential customers" or "our TAM is $X billion" shoehorned into the traction slot. Investors hear that constantly and it doesn't land.

**What to do instead:** Take the time you would have spent on traction and put it into a stronger closing. The closing is your last shot to leave investors with something compelling. The pitch guide says to pick ONE of these:

- Something you understand about the carbon/sustainability market that most people don't
- Your personal experience with the problem CarbonTrace solves
- A significant previous accomplishment that's relevant
- Something that changed recently (regulation, technology, market shift) that makes CarbonTrace possible now
- Your personal expertise in this space

**If you do have pre-launch milestones worth mentioning:** The guide specifically calls out that bio/hard tech companies (and this applies to any pre-launch company with real technical progress) should mention significant milestones — especially if you can put them in context. Did you build something faster or cheaper than alternatives? Have you accomplished something technically that hasn't been done before? Frame it so the listener is impressed, but don't call it "traction."

For example, the difference between:

> "We've built a working prototype." (weak — no context)

vs.

> "In 8 weeks we built a working carbon tracking engine that processes supply chain data 10x faster than the manual audits companies pay $200K/year for." (strong — time context, concrete comparison, implies market pain)

The key principle from the guide: **the time context matters as much as the number.** Even for pre-launch milestones, frame what you did against how long it took and what the alternative looks like.
