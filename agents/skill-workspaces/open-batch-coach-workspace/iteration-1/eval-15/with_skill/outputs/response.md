I hear you, and I get the instinct — you know the investors are technical, so you want to speak their language. But this is actually one of the most common traps in pitch prep, and the YC guide calls it out directly.

Here's the thing: even technical investors don't want a technical pitch at Demo Day. They want to understand what VectorDB does, why it matters, and why it's working — in plain language. You have 2 minutes in front of 100+ people. Even if every single person in that room has a CS degree, they're hearing pitch after pitch back to back. The pitches that land are the ones that are instantly clear, not the ones that flex architecture knowledge.

The guide puts it well: your two-sentence description should create a picture in the listener's mind. Compare these two approaches:

- **Technical:** "VectorDB provides a horizontally-scalable, low-latency vector similarity search engine with ANN indexing optimized for high-dimensional embedding spaces"
- **Clear:** Something like Stripe's approach — "Stripe is the easiest way for developers to process payments online. In just 7 lines of code, you can start accepting payments."

The Stripe example is talking to a technical audience too — developers. But it doesn't describe the architecture. It describes what you get and why it's better. A technical investor hears that and immediately understands the value prop. They'll ask about the architecture in the meeting afterward — which is exactly the goal. Demo Day is about getting that meeting.

So instead of making the pitch more technical, let's make it more concrete and specific. That's what actually impresses technical people — specificity, not jargon.
